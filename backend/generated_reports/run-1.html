Here are at least three logical fallacies in the argument, along with why they weaken it:

1. **Ad hominem / Motive fallacy**  
   - **Claim:** “Our competitors are pushing for AI regulation because they are scared of our innovation.”  
   - **Why it’s fallacious:** This attacks the competitors’ motives instead of addressing whether regulation itself might be reasonable. Even if competitors were motivated by fear or self-interest, that would not prove regulation is bad.  
   - **Why it weakens the argument:** It replaces evidence-based reasoning with speculation about intent.

2. **Slippery slope**  
   - **Claim:** “If we regulate AI now, we are essentially inviting a digital dark age.”  
   - **Why it’s fallacious:** This assumes that regulation will inevitably lead to an extreme negative outcome without showing the steps in between. Not all regulation destroys innovation; some regulation can shape or improve it.  
   - **Why it weakens the argument:** It exaggerates consequences instead of proving them, making the reasoning alarmist rather than logical.

3. **Genetic fallacy / Guilt by association**  
   - **Claim:** “Every major tech leader who supports regulation also has a history of failed product launches.”  
   - **Why it’s fallacious:** This dismisses support for regulation based on who supports it and their past failures, rather than evaluating the merits of their position. Someone can have failed products and still be correct about policy.  
   - **Why it weakens the argument:** It avoids engaging with the actual case for regulation.

4. **Hasty generalization**  
   - **Claim:** “Every major tech leader who supports regulation also has a history of failed product launches.”  
   - **Why it’s fallacious:** This makes a broad claim about “every” major tech leader without evidence. It may rely on selective examples rather than a full, representative sample.  
   - **Why it weakens the argument:** Overgeneralized claims are easy to challenge and undermine credibility.

5. **False dilemma**  
   - **Implied claim:** Either we avoid regulation or we face a “digital dark age.”  
   - **Why it’s fallacious:** It presents only two extreme options, ignoring middle-ground possibilities such as limited, targeted, or adaptive regulation.  
   - **Why it weakens the argument:** It oversimplifies a complex policy issue.

Overall, the argument is weak because it relies on attacking people, speculating about motives, and exaggerating consequences instead of providing evidence about whether AI regulation would actually help or harm innovation.

GPT-5.4

```python
def two_sum(nums, target):
    seen = {}
    for i, num in enumerate(nums):
        complement = target - num
        if complement in seen:
            return [seen[complement], i]
        seen[num] = i
    return []
```

### How it works
- Use a dictionary `seen` to store numbers we've already visited and their indices.
- For each number, compute the `complement = target - num`.
- If that complement is already in `seen`, we found the pair.
- Return their indices immediately.

### Example
```python
print(two_sum([2, 7, 11, 15], 9))  # [0, 1]
```

### Time complexity

- **O(n)** space
